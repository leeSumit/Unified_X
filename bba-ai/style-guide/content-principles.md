# Content Principles — Campus AI Pedagogy

This document captures the philosophical underpinning of all Campus AI content. Every piece of content — notes, workbooks, slides — must embody these three pillars. They are not aspirational; they are structural requirements.

---

## Pillar 1: Design Thinking (IDEO / Stanford d.school)

**The principle**: Students learn by doing, not by reading about doing.

Every module's hands-on work follows the five-stage design thinking process:
1. Empathise — understand the user/stakeholder affected
2. Define — reframe the problem as "How might we…"
3. Ideate — generate multiple possible solutions
4. Prototype — actually build and test the solution
5. Test — design a rigorous evaluation plan

**How this shapes content**:
- Boot Camps teach tools through problems, not definitions
- Projects give students a real scenario before they know the answer
- The Capstone forces a full design thinking sprint in 2 hours
- Discussion questions in Case Studies push to "what would you do?"

**In workbooks**: Every activity should have a "Your work" box — not "summarise the reading" but "apply this to a real company"

---

## Pillar 2: Experiential Learning (Kolb's Cycle, 1984)

**The principle**: Learning happens in a loop — Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualisation → Active Experimentation → back to Concrete Experience.

**How this shapes content**:
- Chapters open with a story (Concrete Experience) before any framework
- The framework follows (Abstract Conceptualisation)
- Mini examples and failure cases provide Reflective Observation
- Projects force Active Experimentation
- The Reflection section closes the loop — "what did you actually build?"

**In notes**: Do not open a chapter with a definition. Open with an event, an anecdote, or a surprising fact. The student must feel the problem before they learn the solution.

**In workbooks**: The "Reflect before you leave class" prompt after each Boot Camp is the Kolb close. It must ask about the student's actual experience in class, not about the theory.

---

## Pillar 3: Industry-First Content

**The principle**: No framework is taught without a company using it today. Theory divorced from practice is forgettable. Theory with a company name, a revenue figure, and a named human behind it — that is remembered.

**How this shapes content**:
- Every framework introduced in a Boot Camp must be immediately followed by "Here is who uses this and how"
- Case Studies are full-length company stories, not abstract examples
- Salary/career data is India-specific and sourced (Glassdoor, Naukri, LinkedIn)
- The Capstone's fictional companies are grounded in real Indian market contexts (Tier-2 cities, realistic ARR, actual Indian consumer patterns)

**In notes**: Three specific companies per module must be deeply featured. Not mentioned in passing — full paragraphs with dates, decisions, and outcomes.

**In workbooks**: The three "Industry-First" companies appear on the Pedagogy page and are woven throughout every Boot Camp example and Case Study.

## The "Stakes" Rule

Every concept must have stakes. The student must understand: what goes wrong if you do not understand this?

Bad: "Prompt engineering is the practice of designing inputs for AI systems."
Good: "Swiggy's customer support team discovered that vague prompts cost them ₹12 lakh per month in incorrect refund approvals. The fix was not a new AI system — it was one week of prompt rewriting."

The stakes can be:
- A real loss (financial, reputational, time)
- A missed opportunity (the competitor who did it right)
- A career risk (the interview question you cannot answer)
- An ethical failure (harm caused by AI doing the wrong thing)

---

## The "Name and Date" Rule

Generalities are forgettable. Specifics are sticky.

Bad: "A large e-commerce company deployed an AI chatbot and saw significant improvement."
Good: "Meesho deployed a WhatsApp-based AI assistant for Tier-3 resellers in Q3 2023. Within six months, their first-contact resolution rate rose from 34% to 71%."

If you do not have a real number, say so honestly: "Specific figures are not public, but the team reported…"

Never invent numbers. Real figures, sourced or clearly attributed, are non-negotiable.

---

## The "No Framework Without a Failure" Rule

Every framework must be taught alongside its failure mode. This does two things:
1. It makes the framework memorable (fear is a better anchor than success)
2. It makes students honest practitioners, not cargo-culters

For each framework taught:
- Name the most common way people apply it wrong
- Show the output of that mistake
- Contrast it with the correct application

This is why every Boot Camp includes a "failure taxonomy" — not to be pessimistic, but to be honest about craft.

## The Career North Star

First-year BBA students are paying for a credential and a career, not for intellectual curiosity. Keep one eye on employability at all times.

Every module must answer, implicitly or explicitly:
- "What job titles hire for this skill?"
- "What does this look like on a CV?"
- "What would you say in an interview about this?"
- "What does a person who mastered this module earn?"

Career anchors should appear:
- In the Introduction (Learning Outcomes frame → career outcomes)
- In the Chapter Close of at least one notes chapter per module
- Explicitly in the Reflection section (Career Pathway page)
- In Case Study Discussion questions (Q4 is always personal/career stance)

## Module Design Constraints

These are hard constraints, not guidelines:

| Constraint | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| Sessions | Always 5 sessions × 2 hours = 10 hours (2 extra hours = preparation/assessment) |
| Boot Camps | Always 2 per module, one per odd session |
| Projects | Always 2 per module, one per even session |
| Case Studies | Always 2 per module — Session 2 = Indian company, Session 4 = Global company |
| Capstone | Always Session 5, always Design Thinking 5-stage format |
| Frameworks per module | 4–6 frameworks/tools minimum |
| India case study | Must be a company students recognise and might aspire to join |
| Global case study | Must include a reversal, failure, or unexpected outcome |
| Crossword | 10 terms, drawn from the module's core vocabulary |

---

## Content Sequencing Logic

The five sessions follow a deliberate learning gradient:

- **Session 1 (Boot Camp 1)**: Foundation concepts — the "why" and the "what"
- **Session 2 (Project 1 + Case Study 1)**: First application — familiar Indian context
- **Session 3 (Boot Camp 2)**: Advanced tools — the "how to do it well"
- **Session 4 (Project 2 + Case Study 2)**: Deeper application — global context, higher stakes
- **Session 5 (Capstone)**: Synthesis — everything at once, under time pressure

Notes chapters should follow this same gradient. Do not front-load all theory. Sequence concepts so each chapter assumes knowledge from the chapter before.
